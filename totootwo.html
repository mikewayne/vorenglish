To vs Too vs Two by Peter Quince

I want to know how to use 'to', 'too' and 'two', too! Hmmm, that’s almost a tongue twister, but not quite. Many students struggle with remembering when to use ‘to’ and when to use ‘too.’

The number 'two' (2) is usually used correctly, but we will include it as part of this lesson.

The word 'to' means going toward something; in the direction of (if being used as a preposition).

This street leads to the highway.

He went to the store.

Give this card to your sister.

The word 'to' also means until.

The course will run from July 1 to August 1.

You can also use 'to' before the basic from of a verb to form the infinitive (to go, to shop, to see etc.)

I am going to go home now.

Helen and Naz want to see a movie at the theatre.

NOTE: The infinitive is NEVER formed with an –ing verb, so never say/write: He went to seeing a movie. ✘

Rather, this sentence is correct:

He went to see a movie.

The word 'too' -- which is an adverb -- means also; as well; in addition.

Jasmine wants to go, too!

"Me, too!" exclaimed the small boy excitedly.

The word 'too' also means excessive; in excess; more than is necessary.

“You are driving way too fast!” said my mother.

Too many candies are bad for your teeth.

A trick to remember when to use “too”: Too has two (2) o's ('oo') which is excessive, way too many, don’t you think?!?

So, when you say/write about something being too much or in addition to something else, think “TOO”!

And now the number two:

Two deer ran across the road, too.

The two men tried to lift the piano, but could not.
